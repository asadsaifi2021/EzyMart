Welcome to EzyMart

EzyMart is a solution to simplify grocery shopping by offering a convenient and easy access to fresh produce for the local buyers.

Features

Product Browsing and Discovery

Visually appealing interface with horizontal sliders for featured products and offers. Items are categorized (Fruits, Vegetables, Dairy, Bakery, Meat, Grocery) with dedicated screens. Each product shows price, origin, availability, nutrition, and real-time stock info

Shopping Cart System

Ability to add products to a shopping cart with quantity selection. View, edit quantities, and remove items from the cart. Cart contents are preserved across sessions, even for guest users. Prevents adding more items than available in stock

Search and Filtering System

Powerful search bar allowing users to find products by name, category, or description. Filters for price range, category, availability, and other product attributes. Ability to sort products by price (low to high and high to low)

Wishlist

Add products to a Wishlist for future consideration. View and manage Wishlist items. Easily move items from Wishlist to cart

User Account System

Create accounts with username, email, and password. Secure login system with credential validation. Ability to browse and add items to cart without an account. Maintains user state across the application with automatic cart transfer when guests log in

Tips & Advice

Contextual tips and advice for better shopping experience. Suggestions for product usage and storage. Tips related to seasonal products and offers

Item Availability

Know what’s in stock before you shop — with real-time product availability updates

Notifications System

Notifications for order status changes. Notifications when Wishlist items become available. Notifications about special offers and promotions. Centralized location to view all notifications

Order History

Streamlined checkout flow for finalizing purchases. Detailed order summary before confirmation. View past orders with details of purchased items, dates, and totals

Nutritional Facts

Comprehensive nutritional information for food products. Clear presentation of calories, macronutrients, vitamins, and minerals. Enables users to make informed dietary choices

FAQ System

FAQ section helps users quickly find answers to common questions. Organized by topic, it guides users in navigating and interacting with the app efficiently. FAQ content is stored in the database for easy management and updates

Our Vision

EzyMart is a solution to simplify grocery shopping by offering a convenient and easy access to fresh produce for the local buyers. By providing a pleasant online shopping experience, EzyMart allows customers to browse, select, and purchase quality products from the comfort of their homes or workplaces.

Audience

EzyMart is designed to serve the users as buyers within its local community through a dedicated delivery service. Users outside the delivery area can browse and explore product listings for informational purposes or personal planning.

Values

The use of such digital shopping, like EzyMart, is always a critical improvement in a society where there are groups of people with traveling disabilities who struggle to go to supermarkets to buy daily goods. Not just a solution to such critical problems, it is also a convenient tool for anyone wanting to reduce the time and effort spent on grocery shopping. Such features include a search bar and an account-based system that secures and personalizes customers' accounts to promote a positive and engaging user experience.

Beyond just convenience, EzyMart prioritizes sustainability and community impact. By providing the item information online and an optimized delivery service model, the app helps lower carbon emissions associated with individual travel. Furthermore, EzyMart provides an outlet for local farmers to consumers, simplifying the partnership model by eliminating the friction of logistical hurdles like having a storefront vs. in-house inventory management.

Success Measures

The project is considered to be successful if all the features are implemented and cover all the requirements of a customer in a shopping session. Time execution from users will also be accounted for. Customers should spend 50% less time, compared to shopping in person, creating their final cart.

The project is considered to be successful if the customers’ overall experience and effectiveness are drastically improved compared to the real-life experience of shopping in the supermarket. In particular, we will mainly account for the execution time from the user to create their final cart and make an order in EzyMart. We will collect the relevant data from the team members’ friends and family members, who have no computer science background and are unfamiliar with EzyMart. We expect the number of testers to be around 15 and the average amount of time is 50% less compared to average time shopping in person.

Frequently Asked Questions

01

What was the overall architecture of your system?

Architecture Style: Three-Tier Architecture with Service Locator Pattern

Layer 1 - Presentation Layer (presentation package): Contains UI components like fragments, activities, and adapters that handle user interactions and display data.

Layer 2 - Business Layer (business package): Includes handler classes that implement business logic and rules, acting as a bridge between the presentation and persistence layers.

Layer 3 - Persistence Layer (persistence package): Provides data access through well-defined interfaces with multiple implementations.

Service Locator Pattern: A key architectural feature is the Service Locator Pattern implemented through the Services class. It acts as a centralized registry for accessing persistence layer implementations. This pattern offers a form of dependency injection that decouples the business layer from specific persistence implementations.

05

Did any features work better than expected?

Features That Exceeded Expectations: Several features turned out to be more robust, seamless, or user-friendly than initially anticipated.

Database Fault Tolerance: Our system demonstrated sophisticated error recovery by automatically detecting failures, falling back to alternate persistence methods, and self-healing without requiring any user intervention. This significantly improved the reliability of the app.

Offline Functionality: We were surprised by how smoothly the app performed offline. Non-authenticated users could browse and manage their cart even without an internet connection, resulting in a more resilient and accessible shopping experience.

Session Management: The transition between guest and authenticated user states worked flawlessly. The automatic cart transfer on login ensured that users never lost their progress, enhancing overall usability.

Search System: Our search feature evolved across multiple iterations and became a powerful tool for product discovery. Its filtering capabilities were intuitive and exceeded our original expectations in terms of both performance and user experience.

Nutrition Information: We used the decorator pattern to elegantly layer nutritional details onto products. This enhanced the user experience without disrupting the core system architecture, proving both clean and extensible in design.

03

What did you learn about team or large project development? What will you start doing, keep doing, or stop doing next time?

One of the most unexpected and valuable lessons we learned as a group during this course was that your program can always have errors, no matter how simple it seems. At the beginning of the term, we intentionally designed a relatively small and straightforward application, avoiding complex or novel features. Our goal was to ensure we could deliver all promised functionalities reliably. However, we soon realized that even simple features can become complicated when integrated across all three architecture layers—business, logic, and data.

Start doing: We will allocate more time for feature integration and thorough testing at each layer. We’ll also perform incremental testing more frequently, rather than assuming that a working stub means the feature is "done." This will help us identify issues earlier in the development process and improve the stability of our codebase.

Keep doing: We’ll continue our practice of early planning and scoping down features to keep the project manageable. This approach helped us stay organized and focused, even when unexpected bugs or setbacks occurred. Clear project boundaries also made it easier to assign tasks and track progress as a team.

Stop doing: We will stop assuming that a feature is complete just because it works in isolation. Instead, we’ll treat integration and full-stack behavior as a critical part of the development cycle. A feature must function correctly within the broader system context before we consider it finished.

Overall, this project taught us to expect the unexpected in team-based software development. It reinforced the importance of collaboration, testing, and flexibility. Most importantly, we learned to always leave room for debugging—no matter how confident we feel about a piece of code.

04

Are there any particular design smells, or brilliant design decisions?

Design Smells: While our architecture had strengths, several design issues were identified during development.

Service Locator Complexity: The Services class violates the Single Responsibility Principle by handling multiple concerns such as connection management, persistence creation, and error handling. This made it harder to test and maintain.

Dependency Inversion Issues: The business layer directly depends on the Services class rather than using proper dependency injection, resulting in tighter coupling and reduced flexibility.

Inconsistent Error Handling: We observed a mix of null returns, boolean indicators, and exceptions across the codebase, which led to inconsistent behavior and made debugging more challenging.

Inheritance Over Composition: Some classes relied on inheritance where composition would have provided better modularity and more maintainable code.

Brilliant Design Decisions: Despite the above, the project featured a number of thoughtful architectural choices that greatly benefited development.

Three-Tier Architecture: We maintained a clear separation between presentation, business logic, and data access layers, which helped keep the codebase organized and scalable.

Multiple Persistence Implementations: Our system supported HSQLDB, stubs, and SharedPreferences with runtime switching, making the application flexible and easy to test in different environments.

Robust Fault Tolerance: We implemented sophisticated error recovery and automatic fallback mechanisms, improving reliability and user experience.

Extension Mechanism: Clean extension points allowed us to add new features without modifying the core architecture, which is a hallmark of good design.

Session Management: The transition between guest and authenticated user states was seamless, ensuring smooth user experience across different app states.

06

Discuss the area you identified for improvement after your Iteration 2 retrospective; how did it go?

Balancing Technical Development and Documentation: After our Iteration 2 retrospective, we recognized the need to better balance technical development with documentation and improve team communication.

Progress in Documentation: In our final iteration, we made significant improvements—our documentation became more polished, the GitLab repository was better structured, and our commit messages and comments improved overall code clarity.

Impact on Technical Progress: While shifting some developers and testers to focus on documentation improved organization, it came at a cost. This reallocation of resources slowed technical progress, ultimately leading to the removal of the Coupons feature.

Challenges in Communication: Initially, our team had strong communication and motivation. However, under pressure, check-ins became less frequent, leading to some confusion in task coordination and decision-making.

Final Outcomes: Despite these challenges, we successfully delivered 11 out of 12 planned features with full testing. However, we identified some violations of SOLID principles, particularly the Single Responsibility Principle (SRP) and Dependency Inversion Principle (DIP), highlighting the need for more frequent mid-iteration code reviews.

Key Takeaways: The iteration reinforced our ability to adapt and work as a team, but balancing priorities between development, documentation, and communication remains an area for growth in future projects.
